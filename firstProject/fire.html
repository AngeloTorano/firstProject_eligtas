It is a catastrophic event where a fire breaks out in a residential setting, leading to extensive damage to property and posing significant risks to the safety of occupants. Such disasters can arise from various sources, including electrical malfunctions, cooking accidents, heating equipment failures, or careless behavior. The consequences of a home fire can be devastating, resulting in loss of life, injuries, and the destruction of personal belongings and family heirlooms. Additionally, the emotional toll on families can be profound, as they grapple with the aftermath of their losses and the disruption of their lives. 

Prepare NOW

  • Create a fire escape plan for your home and share it with every household member.

  • Make sure you have quickly accessible fire extinguishers in your residence.

  • Unplug electrical devices that are not in use.

  • Regularly inspect your home’s electrical and heating systems for any damage like frayed cords, cracks, etc.

  • Keep inflammable items, such as matches and lighters, out of children's reach.

Survive DURING

  • Drop down to the floor and crawl low, under any smoke to your exit.

  • If closed doors or handles are warm, use an alternate exit.

  • If you’re trapped inside, cover the cracks around the door with anything. If you can find something wet, use that. Then, call 911 immediately if you can access a phone.

  • If your clothes catch fire, stop, drop, and roll.

  • Once you’re outside, run away from the house and look for a safe place. Call someone for help immediately.

Be Safe AFTER

  • If your home is damaged and requires you to be displaced, the first step is to find a safe place to stay.

  • Check with the fire department to make sure your residence is safe to enter.

  • DO NOT attempt to reconnect utilities yourself.

  • Conduct an inventory of damaged property and items.
Forest Fire
Forest fires are caused by natural or man-made fires in tropical, temperate and boreal forests. A wildfire must have three conditions in order to burn: fuel, oxygen and a heat source. Any flammable item surrounding a fire, such as trees, grasses, bushes and even dwellings, is considered fuel. A fire requires oxygen to burn, which is provided by air. Heat sources aid in the ignition of the wildfire by raising the temperature of the fuel. Lightning, smoldering campfires or cigarettes, sweltering breezes and even the Sun can all ignite a wildfire. 

Prepare NOW

  • Create defensible space to separate your home from flammable vegetation and materials (minimum 30 ft)

  • Keep roof surfaces clear of pine needles, leaves, and debris at all times.

  • Prune all lower branches 8 feet from the ground.

  • Store all important papers in a fireproof container or keep copies at another location.

  • Make evacuation plans with family members.

Survive DURING

  • Check with fire officials before attempting to return to your home.

  • Check the roof and exterior areas for sparks and embers.

  • When cleaning, wear protective clothing – including a long-sleeved shirt, long pants, work gloves and sturdy thick-soled shoes – during clean-up efforts.

  • Continue to check for problem areas for several days.

Be Safe AFTER

  • If your home is damaged and requires you to be displaced, the first step is to find a safe place to stay.

  • Check with the fire department to make sure your residence is safe to enter.

  • DO NOT attempt to reconnect utilities yourself.

  • Conduct an inventory of damaged property and items.
